## 6.1 INTRODUCTION

Light is mystical. Yet, its behaviour is so fascinating. It is difficult to comprehend light as a single entity. The ray optics deals with light that is represented as a ray travelling in straight lines. Here, the geometrical constructs get the permanence to understand some of the characteristics of light and the phenomena associated with it. There are several other phenomena which can only be explained using wave optics, which we study in the next Unit. There is also a quantum aspect of light which we can study as quantum optics in graduate level courses.

### 6.1.1 Ray optics

Light travels in a straight line in a medium. Light may deviate in its path only when it encounters the boundary of another medium (or) an obstacle. A ray of light gives information only about the direction of light. It does not give information about the other characteristics like intensity and colour of light. However, a ray is a sensible representation of light in ray optics. The path of the light is called a ray of light and a bundle of such rays is called a beam of light. In this chapter, we can explain the phenomena of reflection, refraction, dispersion and scattering of light, using the ray depiction of light.

### 6.1.2 Reflection

The bouncing back of light into the same medium when it encounters a reflecting surface is called reflection of light. Any polished surface can reflect light. Mirrors which are silver coated at their back can reflect almost {{< katex display=true >}}90\%$ of the light falling on them. The angle of incidence $i$ and the angle of reflection $r{{< /katex >}} are measured with respect to the normal drawn to the surface at the point of incidence of light. According to laws of reflection,

(i) The incident ray, reflected ray and normal to the reflecting surface are all coplanar (ie. lie in the same plane).  
(ii) The angle of incidence {{< katex display=true >}}i$ is equal to the angle of reflection $r{{< /katex >}}.

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
i = r \tag{6.1}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

![Reflection of light](image-placeholder)

The laws of reflection are valid at each point for any reflecting surface whether the surface is flat (or) curved. If the reflecting surface is flat, then incident parallel rays after reflection come out as parallel rays. If the reflecting surface is irregular, then the incident parallel rays after reflection come out as irregular rays (not parallel rays). Still the laws of reflection are valid at every point of incidence in irregular reflection.

![Regular and irregular reflections](image-placeholder)

### 6.1.3 Angle of deviation due to reflection

The angle between the direction of incident ray and the reflected ray is called angle of deviation due to reflection. It is calculated by a simple geometry. The incident light is AO. The reflected light is OB. The un-deviated light is OC which is the continuation of the incident light. The angle between OB and OC is the angle of deviation {{< katex display=true >}}d$. From the geometry, it is written as, $d = 180 - (i + r)$. As $i = r{{< /katex >}} in reflection, we can write angle of deviation in reflection as,

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
d = 180 - 2i \tag{6.2}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

The angle of deviation can also be measured in terms of the glancing angle {{< katex display=true >}}\alpha$ which is measured between the incident ray AO and the reflecting plane surface XY. By geometry, the angles $\angle AOX = \alpha$, $\angle BOY = \alpha$ and $\angle YOC = \alpha$ (all are same). The angle of deviation $d$ is the angle $\angle BOC{{< /katex >}}. Therefore,

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
d = 2\alpha \tag{6.3}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

![Angle of deviation due to reflection](image-placeholder)

**EXAMPLE 6.1**  
Prove that for the same incident light when a reflecting surface is tilted by an angle {{< katex display=true >}}\theta$, the reflected light will be tilted by an angle $2\theta{{< /katex >}}.

**Solution**  
AB is the reflecting surface. Both the incident ray IO and the reflected ray {{< katex display=true >}}OR_1$ subtend angle $i$ with the normal $N$ as the angle of incidence is equal to angle of reflection. When the surface AB is tilted to $A'B'$ by an angle $\theta$, the normal $N$ is also tilted to $N'$ by the same angle $\theta$. Remember that the position of the incident ray IO remains unaltered. But the reflected ray now is $OR_2{{< /katex >}}.

Now, in the tilted system, the angle of incidence, {{< katex display=true >}}\angle N'OI = i + \theta$ and the angle of reflection, $\angle N'OR_2 = i + \theta$ are the same. The angle between $ON'$ and $OR_1$ is, $\angle N'OR_1 = i - \theta$. The angle tilted on the reflected light is the angle between $OR_1$ and $OR_2$ which is $\angle R_1OR_2{{< /katex >}}. From the geometry we can write,

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\angle R_1OR_2 = \angle N'OR_2 - \angle N'OR_1 = (i + \theta) - (i - \theta) = 2\theta
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

### 6.1.4 Image formation in plane mirror

Let us consider a point object {{< katex display=true >}}A$ placed in front of a plane mirror. The point of incidence is $O$ on the mirror. A light ray AO from the point object is incident on the mirror and it is reflected along OB. The normal is ON. The angle of incidence $\angle AON =$ angle of reflection $\angle BON{{< /katex >}}.

Another ray AD incident normally on the mirror at {{< katex display=true >}}D$ is reflected back along DA. When BO and AD are extended backwards, they meet at a point $A'$. Thus, the rays appear to come from a point $A'{{< /katex >}} which is behind the plane mirror. The object and its image are at equal perpendicular distances from the plane mirror which can be shown by the following explanation.

Angle {{< katex display=true >}}\angle AON =$ angle $\angle DAO{{< /katex >}} [Since they are alternate angles]  
Angle {{< katex display=true >}}\angle BON =$ angle $\angle OA'D{{< /katex >}} [Since they are corresponding angles]  

Hence, it follows that angle {{< katex display=true >}}\angle DAO =$ $\angle OA'D{{< /katex >}}  

The triangles {{< katex display=true >}}\Delta ODA$ and $\Delta ODA'{{< /katex >}} are congruent  

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\therefore AD = A'D
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

This shows that the image distance {{< katex display=true >}}d_i$ inside the plane mirror is equal to the object distance $d_o{{< /katex >}} in front of the plane mirror.

![Formation of image in plane mirror for point and extended objects](image-placeholder)

### 6.1.5 Characteristics of the image formed by plane mirror

(i) The image formed by a plane mirror is virtual, erect and laterally inverted sidewise (left/right).  
(ii) The size of the image is equal to the size of the object.  
(iii) The image distance behind the mirror is equal to the object distance in front of the mirror.  
(iv) If an object is placed between two plane mirrors inclined at an angle {{< katex display=true >}}\theta$, then the number of images $n{{< /katex >}} formed is given in Table 6.1.

**Table 6.1 Image by inclined mirrors**

| {{< katex display=true >}}360/\theta$ | Position of object placed | Number of images $n{{< /katex >}} |
|--------------|---------------------------|---------------------|
| Even | Symmetrical | {{< katex display=true >}}n = (360/\theta - 1){{< /katex >}} |
| Even | Unsymmetrical | {{< katex display=true >}}n = (360/\theta - 1){{< /katex >}} |
| Odd | Symmetrical | {{< katex display=true >}}n = (360/\theta - 1){{< /katex >}} |
| Odd | Unsymmetrical | {{< katex display=true >}}n = (360/\theta){{< /katex >}} |

## 6.2 SPHERICAL MIRRORS

We shall now study about the reflections that take place in spherical surfaces.

A spherical surface is a part cut from a hollow sphere. Spherical mirrors are generally constructed using glass. One surface of the glass is silvered. The reflection takes place at the other surface which is polished. If the polished surface of the mirror is convex it is called as convex mirror.

We shall get familiarised with some of the terminologies pertaining to spherical mirrors.

![Spherical mirrors](image-placeholder)

**Centre of curvature:** The centre of the sphere of which the mirror is a part is called the centre of curvature {{< katex display=true >}}C{{< /katex >}} of the mirror.

**Radius of curvature:** The radius of the sphere of which the spherical mirror is a part is called the radius of curvature {{< katex display=true >}}R{{< /katex >}} of the mirror.

**Pole:** The middle point on the spherical surface of the mirror (or) the geometrical centre of the mirror is called pole {{< katex display=true >}}P{{< /katex >}} of the mirror (or) optic centre.

**Principal axis:** The line joining the pole {{< katex display=true >}}P$ and the centre of curvature $C{{< /katex >}} is called the principal axis of the mirror. The light ray travelling along the principal axis towards the mirror after reflection travels back along the same principal axis. It is also called optical axis.

### 6.2.1 Paraxial Rays and Marginal Rays

**Focus (or) Focal point:** Light rays travelling parallel and close to the principal axis when incident on a spherical mirror, converge at a point for concave mirror (or) appear to diverge from a point for convex mirror on the principal axis. This point is called the focus (or) focal point {{< katex display=true >}}F{{< /katex >}} of the mirror.

**Focal length:** The distance between the pole {{< katex display=true >}}P$ and the focus $F$ is called the focal length $f{{< /katex >}} of the mirror.

**Focal plane:** The plane through the focus and perpendicular to the principal axis is called the focal plane of the mirror.

All the above mentioned terms are shown for both concave and convex mirrors.

![Representation of terminologies for concave and convex mirrors](image-placeholder)

The paraxial rays are the rays which travel very close to the principal axis and make small angles with it. They fall on the mirror very close to the pole. On the other hand, the marginal rays are the rays which travel far away from the principal axis and make large angles with it. They fall on the mirror far away from the pole. These two rays behave differently (get focused at different points). In this chapter, we shall restrict our studies only to paraxial rays. As the angles made by the paraxial rays are very small, we can make good approximations.

![Paraxial and marginal rays](image-placeholder)

### 6.2.2 Relation between {{< katex display=true >}}f$ and $R{{< /katex >}}

Let {{< katex display=true >}}C$ be the centre of curvature of the mirror. Consider a ray of light parallel to the principal axis is incident on the mirror at $M$. It passes through the principal focus $F$ after reflection. The geometry related to this is shown. The line $CM$ is the normal to the mirror at $M$. Let $i{{< /katex >}} be the angle of incidence and the same will be the angle of reflection.

![Relation between {{< katex display=true >}}f$ and $R{{< /katex >}}](image-placeholder)

If {{< katex display=true >}}MP$ is the perpendicular from $M{{< /katex >}} to the principal axis, then

The angles {{< katex display=true >}}\angle MCP = i$ and $\angle MFP = 2i{{< /katex >}}  
From right angle triangles {{< katex display=true >}}\Delta MCP$ and $\Delta MFP{{< /katex >}} we can write,

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\tan i = \frac{PM}{PC} \quad \text{and} \quad \tan 2i = \frac{PM}{PF}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

As the angles are small, {{< katex display=true >}}\tan i \approx i$ and $\tan 2i \approx 2i{{< /katex >}},

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
i = \frac{PM}{PC} \quad \text{and} \quad 2i = \frac{PM}{PF}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

Simplifying further,

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
2\frac{PM}{PC} = \frac{PM}{PF} \quad \Rightarrow \quad 2PF = PC
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}PF$ is focal length $f$ and $PC$ is the radius of curvature $R{{< /katex >}}.

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
2f = R \qquad (\text{or}) \quad f = \frac{R}{2} \tag{6.4}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

Equation (6.4) is the relation between {{< katex display=true >}}f$ and $R{{< /katex >}}. The construction is shown for convex mirror as well.

### 6.2.3 Image formation in spherical mirrors

The image formed by spherical mirror can be found by ray construction called image tracing. To locate an image point, a minimum of two rays must meet at that point. We can use at least any two of the following four rays.

![Image tracing](image-placeholder)

(i) A ray parallel to the principal axis after reflection will pass (or appear to pass) through the principal focus.  
(ii) A ray passing (or appear passing) through the principal focus, after reflection will travel parallel to the principal axis.  
(iii) A ray passing through the centre of curvature retraces its path after reflection as it is a normal incidence.  
(iv) A ray falling on the pole will get reflected as per law of reflection keeping principal axis as the normal.

### 6.2.4 Cartesian sign convention

While tracing the image, we would normally come across the object distance {{< katex display=true >}}u$, the image distance $v$, the object height $h$, the image height $h'$, the focal length $f$ and the radius of curvature $R{{< /katex >}}. A system of signs for these quantities must be followed so that the relations connecting them are consistent in all types of physical situations. We shall follow the Cartesian sign convention which is now widely used. It is given below and also shown.

(i) The Incident light is taken as if it is travelling from left to right (i.e. object on the left of mirror).  
(ii) All the distances are measured from the pole of the mirror (pole is taken as origin).  
(iii) The distances measured to the right of pole along the principal axis are taken as positive.  
(iv) The distances measured to the left of pole along the principal axis are taken as negative.  
(v) Heights measured upwards perpendicular to the principal axis are taken as positive.  
(vi) Heights measured downwards perpendicular to the principal axis, are taken as negative.

![Cartesian sign convention](image-placeholder)

### 6.2.5 Mirror equation

The mirror equation establishes a relation among object distance {{< katex display=true >}}u$, image distance $v$ and focal length $f{{< /katex >}} for a spherical mirror.

An object {{< katex display=true >}}AB$ is considered on the principal axis of a concave mirror beyond the centre of curvature $C$. The image formation is shown. Let us consider three paraxial rays from point $B$ on the object. The first paraxial ray $BD$ travels parallel to the principal axis. It is incident on the concave mirror at $D$, close to the pole $P$. It is reflected back through the focus $F$. The second paraxial ray $BP$ is incident at the pole $P$. It is reflected along $PB'$. The third paraxial ray $BC$ passing through centre of curvature $C$, falls normally on the mirror at $E$. It is reflected back along the same path. The three reflected rays intersect at the point $B'$. A perpendicular drawn as $A'B'{{< /katex >}} to the principal axis gives the real, inverted image.

![Mirror equation](image-placeholder)

As per law of reflection, the angle of incidence {{< katex display=true >}}\angle BPA$ is equal to the angle of reflection $\angle B'PA'{{< /katex >}}.  

The triangles {{< katex display=true >}}\Delta BPA$ and $\Delta B'PA'{{< /katex >}} are similar. Thus, from the rule of similar triangles,

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\frac{A'B'}{AB} = \frac{PA'}{PA} \tag{6.5}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

The other set of similar triangles are {{< katex display=true >}}\Delta DPF$ and $\Delta B'A'F$ ($PD{{< /katex >}} is almost a straight vertical line).

Thus,

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\frac{A'B'}{PD} = \frac{FA'}{FP} \tag{6.6}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

But {{< katex display=true >}}PD = AB{{< /katex >}} as the aperture of the mirror is small. Therefore,

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\frac{A'B'}{AB} = \frac{FA'}{FP} \tag{6.7}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

From equations (6.5) and (6.7),

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\frac{PA'}{PA} = \frac{FA'}{FP} \tag{6.8}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

But {{< katex display=true >}}PA' = -v$, $PA = -u$, $FA' = -v + f$, $FP = -f{{< /katex >}} (sign convention applied). Substituting these in equation (6.8),

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\frac{-v}{-u} = \frac{-v + f}{-f} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \frac{v}{u} = \frac{v - f}{f}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

After rearranging,

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\frac{v}{u} = \frac{v}{f} - 1 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \frac{v}{f} - \frac{v}{u} = 1
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

Dividing throughout by {{< katex display=true >}}v{{< /katex >}},

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\frac{1}{f} - \frac{1}{u} = \frac{1}{v} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \frac{1}{v} + \frac{1}{u} = \frac{1}{f} \tag{6.9}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

The equation (6.9) is called the mirror equation.

### 6.2.6 Lateral magnification in spherical mirrors

The lateral or transverse magnification {{< katex display=true >}}m$ is defined as the ratio of the height of the image $h'$ to the height of the object $h{{< /katex >}}.

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
m = \frac{h'}{h} \tag{6.10}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

From the two similar triangles {{< katex display=true >}}\Delta ABP$ and $\Delta A'B'P{{< /katex >}},

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\frac{A'B'}{AB} = \frac{PA'}{PA} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \frac{h'}{h} = \frac{-v}{-u} = \frac{v}{u} \tag{6.11}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

Therefore,

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
m = \frac{h'}{h} = \frac{v}{u} \tag{6.12}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

Combining the mirror equation (6.9) with (6.12),

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
m = \frac{f - v}{f} = \frac{f}{f - u} \tag{6.13}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

**EXAMPLE 6.3**  
An object is placed at a distance of {{< katex display=true >}}20.0\ \mathrm{cm}$ from a concave mirror of focal length $15.0\ \mathrm{cm}{{< /katex >}}.  
(a) What distance from the mirror a screen should be placed to get a sharp image?  
(b) What is the nature of the image?

**Solution**  
Given, {{< katex display=true >}}f = -15\ \mathrm{cm}$, $u = -20\ \mathrm{cm}{{< /katex >}}

(a) Mirror equation, {{< katex display=true >}}\frac{1}{v} + \frac{1}{u} = \frac{1}{f}{{< /katex >}}

Rewriting to find {{< katex display=true >}}v{{< /katex >}}:

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\frac{1}{v} = \frac{1}{f} - \frac{1}{u}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

Substituting for {{< katex display=true >}}f$ and $u{{< /katex >}},

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\frac{1}{v} = \frac{1}{-15} - \frac{1}{-20} = \frac{-20 + 15}{300} = \frac{-5}{300} = \frac{-1}{60}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
v = -60.0\ \mathrm{cm}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

The screen is to be placed at distance {{< katex display=true >}}60.0{{< /katex >}} cm to the left of the concave mirror.

(b) Magnification, {{< katex display=true >}}m = \frac{h'}{h} = -\frac{v}{u}{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
m = -\frac{(-60)}{(-20)} = -3
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

As the sign of magnification is negative, the image is inverted. As the magnitude of magnification is 3, the image is enlarged three times. As the image is formed to the left of the concave mirror, the image is real.

## 6.3 SPEED OF LIGHT

Light travels with the highest speed in vacuum. The speed of light in vacuum is denoted as {{< katex display=true >}}c$ and its value is $c = 3\times 10^{8}\ \mathrm{ms^{-1}}{{< /katex >}}. The earliest attempt to determine the speed of light was made by a French scientist Hippolyte Fizeau (1819-1896). That paved way for the other scientists too to determine the speed of light.

### 6.3.1 Fizeau's method to determine speed of light

**Apparatus:** The apparatus used by Fizeau for determining speed of light in air medium is shown. The light from the source {{< katex display=true >}}S$ was first allowed to fall on a partially silvered glass plate $G$ kept at an angle of $45^\circ$ to the incident light. The light then was allowed to pass through a rotating toothed-wheel with $N$ teeth and $N$ cuts of equal widths whose speed of rotation could be varied through an external mechanism (not shown). The light passing through one cut in the wheel will get reflected by a mirror $M$ kept at a long distance $d$ about $8\ \mathrm{km}{{< /katex >}} from the toothed-wheel. If the toothed-wheel was not rotating, the light reflected back from the mirror would again pass through the same cut and reach the eyes of the observer who looks through the partially silvered glass plate.

**Working:** The angular speed of rotation of the toothed-wheel was increased from zero to a value {{< katex display=true >}}\omega{{< /katex >}} until the light passing through one cut would completely be blocked by the adjacent tooth. This is ensured by the disappearance of the light while looking through the partially silvered glass plate.

**Expression for speed of light:** The speed {{< katex display=true >}}v$ of light in air is equal to the ratio of the distance $2d$ (the distance light travelled from the toothed-wheel to the mirror and back), to the time taken $t{{< /katex >}}.

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
v = \frac{2d}{t} \tag{6.12}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

The distance {{< katex display=true >}}d$ is a known value from the arrangement. The time $t$ taken for the light to travel the distance $2d$ is calculated from the angular speed $\omega{{< /katex >}} of the toothed-wheel.

The angular speed {{< katex display=true >}}\omega$ (with unit rad $\mathrm{s^{-1}}{{< /katex >}}) of the toothed-wheel when the light disappeared for the first time is,

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\omega = \frac{\theta}{t} \tag{6.13}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

Here, {{< katex display=true >}}\theta$ is the angle between one tooth and the next slot which is turned within that time $t{{< /katex >}}.

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\theta = \frac{\text{total angle of the circle in radian}}{\text{number of teeth + number of cuts}} = \frac{2\pi}{2N} = \frac{\pi}{N}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

Substituting {{< katex display=true >}}\theta{{< /katex >}} in equation (6.13),

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\omega = \frac{\pi/N}{t} = \frac{\pi}{Nt}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

Rewriting the above equation for {{< katex display=true >}}t{{< /katex >}},

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
t = \frac{\pi}{N\omega} \tag{6.14}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

Substituting this in equation (6.12),

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
v = \frac{2d}{\pi/(N\omega)} = \frac{2dN\omega}{\pi} \tag{6.15}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

Fizeau had some difficulty to visually estimate the minimum intensity of the light when it is blocked by the adjacent tooth. The value of speed of light determined by him was very close to the actual value. Later on, with the same idea of Fizeau and with much sophisticated instruments, the speed of light in air was determined as {{< katex display=true >}}v = 2.99792 \times 10^{8}\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}{{< /katex >}}.

![Fizeau's method](image-placeholder)

### 6.3.2 Speed of light through vacuum and different media

Scientists like Foucault (1819-1868) and Michelson (1852-1931) introduced different transparent media like glass, water etc., in the path of light to find the speed of light in different media. Even evacuated glass tubes were also introduced in the path of light to find the speed of light in vacuum. It was found that light travels with lesser speed in any medium than its speed in vacuum. The speed of light in vacuum was determined as {{< katex display=true >}}c = 3 \times 10^{8}\ \mathrm{m\ s^{-1}}{{< /katex >}}. We could notice that the speed of light in vacuum and speed of light in air are almost same.

### 6.3.3 Refractive index

Refractive index of a transparent medium is defined as the ratio of speed of light in vacuum {{< katex display=true >}}c$ to the speed of light in that medium $v{{< /katex >}}.

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\text{refractive index } n \text{ of a medium} = \frac{\text{speed of light in vacuum } (c)}{\text{speed of light in medium } (v)} \tag{6.16}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

### 6.3.4 Optical path

Optical path is defined as the equivalent distance travelled in vacuum in the same time that light takes to travel a distance {{< katex display=true >}}d$ in a medium of refractive index $n$. Let light travel with speed $v$ in a medium of refractive index $n$ and cover a distance $d$ in time $t{{< /katex >}}. Then,

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
d = vt
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

In the same time {{< katex display=true >}}t$, light travels a distance $d'$ in vacuum with speed $c{{< /katex >}}. Then,

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
d' = ct
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

Combining the two equations,

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\frac{d'}{c} = \frac{d}{v} \quad \Rightarrow \quad d' = \frac{c}{v} d = n d \tag{6.17}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

Thus, optical path {{< katex display=true >}}d' = n d{{< /katex >}}.

**EXAMPLE 6.5**  
Light travels from air into a glass slab of thickness {{< katex display=true >}}50\ \mathrm{cm}{{< /katex >}} and refractive index 1.5.  
(a) What is the speed of light in the glass slab?  
(b) What is the time taken by the light to travel through the glass slab?  
(c) What is the optical path of the glass slab?

**Solution**  
Given, thickness of glass slab {{< katex display=true >}}d = 50\ \mathrm{cm} = 0.5\ \mathrm{m}$, refractive index $n = 1.5{{< /katex >}}.

(a) Speed of light in the glass slab is

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
v = \frac{c}{n} = \frac{3\times 10^{8}}{1.5} = 2\times 10^{8}\ \mathrm{ms^{-1}}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

(b) Time taken by light to travel through the glass slab is

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
t = \frac{d}{v} = \frac{0.5}{2\times 10^{8}} = 2.5\times 10^{-9}\ \mathrm{s}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

(c) Optical path

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
d' = n d = 1.5 \times 0.5 = 0.75\ \mathrm{m} = 75\ \mathrm{cm}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

Light would have travelled an additional {{< katex display=true >}}25\ \mathrm{cm}$ ($75\ \mathrm{cm} - 50\ \mathrm{cm}{{< /katex >}}) in vacuum at the same time had there been no glass slab in its path.

## 6.4 REFRACTION

Refraction is passing of light from one optical medium to another optical medium through a boundary. In refraction, the angle of incidence {{< katex display=true >}}i$ in one medium and the angle of refraction $r{{< /katex >}} in the other medium are measured with respect to the normal drawn to the surface at the point of incidence of light. According to laws of refraction,

(i) The incident ray, refracted ray and normal to the refracting surface are all coplanar (ie. lie in the same plane).  
(ii) The ratio of sine of angle of incident {{< katex display=true >}}i$ in the first medium to the sine of angle of refraction $r$ in the second medium is equal to the ratio of refractive index $n_2$ of the second medium to the refractive index $n_1{{< /katex >}} of the first medium.

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\frac{\sin i}{\sin r} = \frac{n_2}{n_1} \tag{6.18}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

The above equation is in the ratio form. It can also be written in a product form as,

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
n_1 \sin i = n_2 \sin r \tag{6.19}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

The law of refraction is also known as Snell's law.

![Refraction of light](image-placeholder)

### 6.4.1 Angle of deviation due to refraction

The angle between the direction of incident ray and the refracted ray is called angle of deviation due to refraction. When light travels from rarer to denser medium, it deviates towards normal. The angle of deviation in this case is {{< katex display=true >}}d = i - r{{< /katex >}}.

![Angle of deviation due to refraction from rarer to denser medium](image-placeholder)

On the other hand, if light travels from denser to rarer medium, it deviates away from normal. The angle of deviation in this case is {{< katex display=true >}}d = r - i{{< /katex >}}.

![Angle of deviation due to refraction from denser to rarer medium](image-placeholder)

### 6.4.2 Simultaneous reflection (or) refraction

In any refracting surface there will also be some reflection taking place. Thus, the intensity of refracted light will be lesser than the incident light. The phenomenon in which a part of light from a source undergoing reflection and the other part of light from the same source undergoing refraction at the same surface is called simultaneous reflection (or) simultaneous refraction. Such surfaces are available as partially silvered glasses.

![Simultaneous reflection (or) refraction](image-placeholder)

### 6.4.3 Principle of reversibility

If a ray of light travels from medium 1 to medium 2 along a certain path, it travels the same path from medium 2 to medium 1. For refraction at a boundary, using Snell's law in product form,

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
n_1 \sin i = n_2 \sin r
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

For the reverse path, the angle of incidence in medium 2 is {{< katex display=true >}}r$ and the angle of refraction in medium 1 is $i{{< /katex >}}. Then,

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
n_2 \sin r = n_1 \sin i
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

Thus, the path is reversible.

### 6.4.4 Relative refractive index

The refractive index of medium 2 with respect to medium 1 is defined as the ratio of refractive index of medium 2 to that of medium 1.

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
n_{21} = \frac{n_2}{n_1} = \frac{\sin i}{\sin r} \tag{6.20}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

Light travelling through transparent oil enters into glass of refractive index 1.5. If the refractive index of glass with respect to the oil is 1.25, what is the refractive index of the oil?

**Solution**  
Given, {{< katex display=true >}}n_{go} = 1.25$ and $n_g = 1.5{{< /katex >}}  
Refractive index of glass with respect to oil, {{< katex display=true >}}n_{go} = \frac{n_g}{n_o}{{< /katex >}}  
Rewriting for refractive index of oil, {{< katex display=true >}}n_o = \frac{n_g}{n_{go}} = \frac{1.5}{1.25} = 1.2{{< /katex >}}  
The refractive index of oil is {{< katex display=true >}}n_o = 1.2{{< /katex >}}.

### 6.4.5 Apparent depth

It is a common observation that the bottom of a tank filled with water appears to be raised when seen from air medium above. An equation could be derived for the apparent depth when viewed in the near normal direction.

Light from the object {{< katex display=true >}}O$ at the bottom of the tank passes from denser medium (water) to rarer medium (air) to reach our eyes for viewing the object. It deviates away from the normal in the rarer medium at the point of incidence $B$. The refractive index of the denser medium is $n_1$ and that of rarer medium is $n_2$. Here $n_1 > n_2$. The angle of incidence in the denser medium is $i$ and the angle of refraction in the rarer medium is $r$. The lines $NN'$ and $OD$ are parallel. Thus, the angle $\angle DIB$ is also $r$. The angles $i$ and $r$ are very small as the diverging light from $O{{< /katex >}} entering the eye is very narrow. The Snell's law in product form from equation (6.19) is,

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
n_1 \sin i = n_2 \sin r
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

As the angles {{< katex display=true >}}i$ and $r$ are small, we can approximate $\sin i \approx \tan i$ and $\sin r \approx \tan r{{< /katex >}}.

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
n_1 \tan i = n_2 \tan r
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

In triangles {{< katex display=true >}}\triangle DOB$ and $\triangle DIB{{< /katex >}},

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\tan i = \frac{DB}{DO}, \quad \tan r = \frac{DB}{DI}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
n_1 \frac{DB}{DO} = n_2 \frac{DB}{DI}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}DB$ cancels both sides. Now, $DO$ is the actual depth $d$ and $DI$ is the apparent depth $d'{{< /katex >}}.

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
n_1 \frac{1}{d} = n_2 \frac{1}{d'} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \frac{d'}{d} = \frac{n_2}{n_1} \tag{6.25}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

Rewriting for apparent depth {{< katex display=true >}}d'{{< /katex >}},

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
d' = \frac{n_2}{n_1} d \tag{6.26}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

As the rarer medium is air, its refractive index {{< katex display=true >}}n_2$ can be taken as 1 ($n_2 = 1$). And the refractive index $n_1$ of denser medium could then be taken as $n$ itself ($n_1 = n{{< /katex >}}). Now, the equation for apparent depth becomes,

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
d' = \frac{d}{n} \tag{6.27}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

The bottom appears to be elevated by {{< katex display=true >}}d - d'{{< /katex >}},

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
d - d' = d - \frac{d}{n} = d\left(1 - \frac{1}{n}\right) \tag{6.28}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

**Atmospheric refraction:** Due to refraction of light through different layers of atmosphere which vary in refractive index, the path of light deviates continuously when it passes through the atmosphere. For example, the Sun is visible a little before the actual sunrise and also until a little after the actual sunset due to refraction of light through the atmosphere. What we mean by actual sunrise is the actual crossing of the sun at the horizon. The apparent shift in the direction of the sun is around half a degree and the corresponding time difference between the actual and apparent positions is about 2 minutes. Sun appears flattened (oval shaped) during sunrise and sunset due to the same phenomenon.

The same is also applicable for the positions of stars. Actually, the stars do not twinkle. They appear twinkling because of the movement of the atmospheric layers with varying refractive indices which is clearly seen in the night sky.

**EXAMPLE 6.7**  
A coin is at the bottom of a trough containing three immiscible liquids of refractive indices 1.3, 1.4 and 1.5 poured one above the other of heights {{< katex display=true >}}30\ \mathrm{cm}$, $16\ \mathrm{cm}$, and $20\ \mathrm{cm}{{< /katex >}} respectively. What is the apparent depth at which the coin appears to be when seen from air medium outside? In which medium the coin will appear?

**Solution**  
When seen from air medium on top, the coin will still appear to be at the bottom with each medium appearing to have shrunk with respect to the air medium outside. This situation is illustrated.

The equations for apparent depth for each medium is,

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
d_1' = \frac{d_1}{n_1}, \quad d_2' = \frac{d_2}{n_2}, \quad d_3' = \frac{d_3}{n_3}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
d' = d_1' + d_2' + d_3' = \frac{d_1}{n_1} + \frac{d_2}{n_2} + \frac{d_3}{n_3}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
d' = \frac{30}{1.3} + \frac{16}{1.4} + \frac{20}{1.5} = 23.1 + 11.4 + 13.3 = 47.8\ \mathrm{cm}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

### 6.4.6 Critical angle and total internal reflection

When a ray passes from an optically denser medium to an optically rarer medium, it bends away from the normal. Because of this, the angle of refraction {{< katex display=true >}}r$ in the rarer medium is greater than the corresponding angle of incidence $i$ in the denser medium. As angle of incidence $i$ is gradually increased, $r$ rapidly increases and at a certain stage $r$ becomes $90^\circ$ and the refracted ray will be gracing the boundary. The angle of incidence in the denser medium for which the angle of refraction is $90^\circ$ (or) the refracted ray graces the boundary between the two media is called critical angle $i_c{{< /katex >}}.

If the angle of incidence in the denser medium is increased beyond the critical angle, there is no refraction possible into the rarer medium. For any angle of incidence greater than the critical angle, the entire light is reflected back into the denser medium itself. This phenomenon is called total internal reflection.

The two conditions for total internal reflection to take place are,
(i) light must travel from denser to rarer medium,
(ii) angle of incidence in the denser medium must be greater than critical angle {{< katex display=true >}}(i > i_c){{< /katex >}}.

For critical angle of incidence, the Snell's law in the product form becomes,

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
n_1 \sin i_c = n_2 \sin 90^\circ \tag{6.29}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
n_1 \sin i_c = n_2 \quad (\because \sin 90^\circ = 1)
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\sin i_c = \frac{n_2}{n_1} \tag{6.30}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

Here {{< katex display=true >}}n_1 > n_2{{< /katex >}}.

If the rarer medium is air, then its refractive index {{< katex display=true >}}n_2$ is 1 ($n_2 = 1$) and the refractive index of the denser medium $n_1$ is taken as $n$ itself ($n_1 = n{{< /katex >}}). Then,

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\sin i_c = \frac{1}{n} \quad (\text{or}) \quad i_c = \sin^{-1}\left(\frac{1}{n}\right) \tag{6.31}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

The critical angle {{< katex display=true >}}i_c$ depends on the refractive index $n{{< /katex >}} of the medium. Table 6.3 shows the refractive index and the critical angle for different materials.

**Table 6.3 Refractive index and critical angle for different media**

| Material | Refractive Index | Critical Angle |
|----------|------------------|----------------|
| Ice | 1.310 | 49.8° |
| Water | 1.333 | 48.6° |
| Fused Quartz | 1.458 | 43.3° |
| Crown Glass | 1.541 | 40.5° |
| Flint Glass | 1.890 | 31.9° |
| Calcite | 1.658 | 37.0° |
| Strontium Titanate | 2.394 | 24.7° |
| Diamond | 2.417 | 24.4° |
| Rutile | 2.621 | 22.4° |

For example, the refractive index of glass is about 1.5. The critical angle for glass-air interface is {{< katex display=true >}}i_c = \sin^{-1}(1/1.5) = 41.8^\circ$. The refractive index of water is 1.33. The critical angle for water-air interface is $i_c = \sin^{-1}(1/1.33) = 48.6^\circ{{< /katex >}}.

### 6.4.7 Effects due to total internal reflection

#### 6.4.7.1 Glittering of diamond

Diamond appears dazzling because of the total internal reflection of light that happens inside the diamond. The refractive index of diamond is about 2.417. It is much greater than the refractive index of ordinary glass which is about only 1.5. The critical angle of diamond is about {{< katex display=true >}}24.4^\circ$. It is much less than that of ordinary glass. A skilled diamond cutter makes use of this larger range of angle of incidence ($24.4^\circ$ to $90^\circ{{< /katex >}} inside the diamond), to ensure that light entering the diamond is total internally reflected from the many cut faces before getting out. This gives a sparkling effect for diamond.

![Total internal reflection in diamond](image-placeholder)

#### 6.4.7.2 Mirage and looming

The refractive index of air increases with its density. In hot places, air near the ground is hotter than air at a height. Hot air is less dense. Hence, in still air the refractive index of air increases with height. Because of this, the light from tall objects like a tree, try to pass through a medium whose refractive index decreases towards the ground. Hence, the ray of light successively deviates away from the normal at different layers of air and undergoes total internal reflection when the angle of incidence near the ground exceeds the critical angle. This gives an illusion as if the light comes from somewhere below the ground. Because of the shaky nature of the layers of air, the observer feels as if the object is getting reflected by a pool of water (or) wet surface beneath the object. This phenomenon is called mirage.

![Mirage and looming](image-placeholder)

In cold places the refractive index increases towards the ground because the temperature of air close to the ground is lesser than the temperature at a height above the surface of earth. Thus, the density and refractive index of air close to the ground is greater than for air at a height. In the cold regions like glaciers and frozen lakes, the reverse effect of mirage will happen. Hence, an inverted image is formed little above the surface. This phenomenon is called looming. It is also called as superior mirage, towering and stooping.

#### 6.4.7.3 Prisms making using of total internal reflection

Prisms can be designed to reflect light by {{< katex display=true >}}90^\circ$ or by $180^\circ{{< /katex >}} by making use of total internal reflection.

![Prisms making use of total internal reflection](image-placeholder)

#### 6.4.7.4 Radius of illumination (Snell's window)

When a source of light like electric bulb is kept inside a water tank, the light from the source travels in all direction inside the water. The light that is incident on the water surface at an angle less than the critical angle will undergo refraction and emerge out from the water. The light incident at an angle greater than critical angle will undergo total internal reflection. The light falling particularly at critical angle graces the surface. Thus, the entire surface of water appears illuminated when seen from outside.

On the other hand, when the light entering the water from outside is seen from inside the water, the view is restricted to a particular angle equal to the critical angle {{< katex display=true >}}i_c$. The restricted illuminated circular area is called Snell's window. The angle of view for water animals is restricted to twice the critical angle $2i_c$. The critical angle for water is $48.6^\circ$. Thus the angle of view is $97.2^\circ$. The radius $R$ of the circular area depends on the depth $d$ from which it is seen and also the refractive index $n$ of the medium. The radius $R{{< /katex >}} of Snell's window can be deduced with the illustration.

![Snell's window and angle of view for water animals](image-placeholder)

Light is seen from a point {{< katex display=true >}}A$ at a depth $d$. The Snell's law in product form for the refraction happening at the point $B{{< /katex >}} on the boundary between the two media is,

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
n_1 \sin i_c = n_2 \sin 90^\circ \quad \Rightarrow \quad n_1 \sin i_c = n_2 \tag{6.32}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\sin i_c = \frac{n_2}{n_1} \tag{6.33}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

From the right angle triangle {{< katex display=true >}}\Delta ABC{{< /katex >}},

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\sin i_c = \frac{CB}{AB} = \frac{R}{\sqrt{d^2 + R^2}} \tag{6.34}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

Equating (6.33) and (6.34),

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\frac{R}{\sqrt{d^2 + R^2}} = \frac{n_2}{n_1}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

Squaring on both sides,

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\frac{R^2}{R^2 + d^2} = \left(\frac{n_2}{n_1}\right)^2
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

Taking reciprocal,

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\frac{R^2 + d^2}{R^2} = \left(\frac{n_1}{n_2}\right)^2
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

Further simplifying,

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
1 + \frac{d^2}{R^2} = \left(\frac{n_1}{n_2}\right)^2 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \frac{d^2}{R^2} = \frac{n_1^2}{n_2^2} - 1 = \frac{n_1^2 - n_2^2}{n_2^2}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

Again taking reciprocal and rearranging,

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\frac{R^2}{d^2} = \frac{n_2^2}{n_1^2 - n_2^2} \quad \Rightarrow \quad R^2 = d^2 \left(\frac{n_2^2}{n_1^2 - n_2^2}\right)
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

After taking the square root, the radius of illumination is,

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
R = d \sqrt{\frac{n_2^2}{n_1^2 - n_2^2}} \tag{6.35}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

If the rarer medium outside is air, then {{< katex display=true >}}n_2 = 1$ and we can take $n_1 = n{{< /katex >}},

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
R = d \left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{n^2 - 1}}\right) \quad \text{or} \quad R = \frac{d}{\sqrt{n^2 - 1}} \tag{6.36}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

**EXAMPLE 6.8**  
What is the radius of the illumination when seen above from inside a swimming pool from a depth of {{< katex display=true >}}10\ \mathrm{m}$ on a sunny day? What is the total angle of view? [Given, refractive index of water is $4/3{{< /katex >}}]

**Solution**  
Given, {{< katex display=true >}}n = 4/3$, $d = 10\ \mathrm{m}{{< /katex >}}

Radius of illumination,

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
R = \frac{d}{\sqrt{n^2 - 1}} = \frac{10}{\sqrt{(4/3)^2 - 1}} = \frac{10 \times 3}{\sqrt{16 - 9}} = \frac{30}{\sqrt{7}} = 11.32\ \mathrm{m}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

To find the critical angle,

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
i_c = \sin^{-1}\left(\frac{1}{n}\right) = \sin^{-1}\left(\frac{1}{4/3}\right) = \sin^{-1}\left(\frac{3}{4}\right) = 48.6^\circ
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

The total angle of view of the cone is,

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
2i_c = 2 \times 48.6^\circ = 97.2^\circ
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

---

#### 6.4.7.5 Optical fibre

Transmitting the signals through optical fibres is possible due to the phenomenon of total internal reflection. Optical fibres consist of inner part called core and outer part called cladding (or) sleaving. The refractive index of the core must be higher than that of the cladding for total internal reflection to happen. Signal in the form of light is made to incident inside the core-cladding boundary at an angle greater than the critical angle. Hence, it advances with repeated total internal reflections inside the optical fibre without undergoing any refraction. The light travels inside the core with no appreciable loss in the intensity of the light.

![Optical fibre](image-placeholder)

While bending the optical fibre, it is done in such a way that the condition for total internal reflection is ensured at every reflection inside the fibre.

#### 6.4.7.6 Acceptance angle in optical fibre

To ensure the critical angle incidence in the core-cladding boundary inside the optical fibre, the light should be incident at a certain angle called acceptance angle at the end of the optical fibre while entering into it. It depends on the refractive indices of the core {{< katex display=true >}}n_1$, cladding $n_2$ and the outer medium $n_3$. Assume that the light is incident at an angle called acceptance angle $i_a$ at the outer medium-core boundary at $A{{< /katex >}}.

![Acceptance angle and acceptance cone](image-placeholder)

The Snell's law in the product form for this refraction at the point {{< katex display=true >}}A{{< /katex >}} is,

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
n_3 \sin i_a = n_1 \sin r_a \tag{6.37}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

To have the total internal reflection inside the optical fibre, the angle of incidence at the core-cladding interface at {{< katex display=true >}}B$ should be at least critical angle $i_c$. Snell's law for the refraction at point $B{{< /katex >}} is,

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
n_1 \sin i_c = n_2 \sin 90^\circ \quad \Rightarrow \quad n_1 \sin i_c = n_2 \tag{6.38}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\sin i_c = \frac{n_2}{n_1} \tag{6.39}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

From the right angle triangle {{< katex display=true >}}\Delta ABC{{< /katex >}},

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
i_c = 90^\circ - r_a
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

Now, equation (6.39) becomes,

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\sin(90^\circ - r_a) = \frac{n_2}{n_1} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \cos r_a = \frac{n_2}{n_1} \tag{6.40}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

From equation (6.37),

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\sin i_a = \frac{n_1}{n_3} \sin r_a \tag{6.41}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

Using {{< katex display=true >}}\sin r_a = \sqrt{1 - \cos^2 r_a}{{< /katex >}} and substituting (6.40) in (6.41),

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\sin i_a = \frac{n_1}{n_3} \sqrt{1 - \left(\frac{n_2}{n_1}\right)^2} = \frac{1}{n_3} \sqrt{n_1^2 - n_2^2} \tag{6.42}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

If the outer medium is air, then {{< katex display=true >}}n_3 = 1$. The acceptance angle $i_a{{< /katex >}} is,

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
i_a = \sin^{-1}\left(\sqrt{n_1^2 - n_2^2}\right) \tag{6.43}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

The numerical aperture (NA) of the optical fibre is defined as {{< katex display=true >}}\sin i_a{{< /katex >}},

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\mathrm{NA} = \sin i_a = \sqrt{n_1^2 - n_2^2} \tag{6.44}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

---

**EXAMPLE 6.9**  
The refractive indices of the core and cladding of an optical fibre are 1.5 and 1.48 respectively. Find the acceptance angle and the numerical aperture of the optical fibre.

**Solution**  
Given, {{< katex display=true >}}n_1 = 1.5$, $n_2 = 1.48$, $n_3 = 1{{< /katex >}} (air).

Numerical aperture,

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\mathrm{NA} = \sqrt{n_1^2 - n_2^2} = \sqrt{1.5^2 - 1.48^2} = \sqrt{2.25 - 2.1904} = \sqrt{0.0596} = 0.244
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

Acceptance angle,

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
i_a = \sin^{-1}(\mathrm{NA}) = \sin^{-1}(0.244) \approx 14.1^\circ
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

### 6.4.8 Lateral displacement in glass slab

When a ray of light passes through a glass slab of thickness {{< katex display=true >}}t$ and refractive index $n{{< /katex >}}, it gets laterally displaced. The lateral displacement depends upon (i) the thickness of the slab, (ii) the angle of incidence and (iii) the refractive index of the slab. The derivation for lateral displacement is as follows.

Consider a ray of light {{< katex display=true >}}PQ$ incident at an angle $i$ on a parallel-sided glass slab of thickness $t$ and refractive index $n$. The ray refracts at $Q$ with angle $r$ and travels inside the slab. At the other face, it emerges with angle $i$ (by principle of reversibility). The lateral displacement $L{{< /katex >}} is the perpendicular distance between the incident ray and the emergent ray.

From geometry, in triangle {{< katex display=true >}}QRS$, $QR = \frac{t}{\cos r}$, and in triangle $QRT$, $L = QR \sin(i - r){{< /katex >}}.

Thus,

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
L = t \frac{\sin(i - r)}{\cos r} \tag{6.50}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

---

**EXAMPLE 6.10**  
The thickness of a glass slab is {{< katex display=true >}}0.25\ \mathrm{m}$. It has a refractive index of 1.5. A ray of light is incident on the surface of the slab at an angle of $60^\circ{{< /katex >}}. Find the lateral displacement of the light when it emerges from the other side of the glass slab.

**Solution**  
Given, {{< katex display=true >}}t = 0.25\ \mathrm{m}$, $n = 1.5$, $i = 60^\circ{{< /katex >}}.

Using Snell's law, {{< katex display=true >}}1 \cdot \sin i = n \sin r{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\sin r = \frac{\sin i}{n} = \frac{\sin 60^\circ}{1.5} = \frac{0.866}{1.5} = 0.5773
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
r = \sin^{-1}(0.5773) \approx 35.25^\circ
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

Lateral displacement,

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
L = t \frac{\sin(i - r)}{\cos r} = 0.25 \times \frac{\sin(60^\circ - 35.25^\circ)}{\cos 35.25^\circ} = 0.25 \times \frac{\sin 24.75^\circ}{\cos 35.25^\circ}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\sin 24.75^\circ \approx 0.418, \quad \cos 35.25^\circ \approx 0.816
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
L = 0.25 \times \frac{0.418}{0.816} \approx 0.128\ \mathrm{m} = 12.8\ \mathrm{cm}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

## 6.5 REFRACTION AT SINGLE SPHERICAL SURFACE

We have so far studied only the refraction at plane surface. The refraction can also take place at spherical surface between two transparent media. The laws of refraction hold good at every point on the spherical surface. The normal at the point of incidence is perpendicular drawn to the tangent plane of the spherical surface at that point. Therefore, the normal always passes through its center of curvature. The study of refraction at single spherical surface paves way to the understanding of thin lenses which consist of two refracting surfaces.

The following assumptions are made while considering refraction at spherical surfaces.
(i) The incident light is assumed to be monochromatic (single colour)
(ii) The incident light is very close to the principal axis (paraxial rays).

The sign conventions are similar to that of the spherical mirrors.

### 6.5.1 Equation for refraction at single spherical surface

Let us consider two transparent media with refractive indices {{< katex display=true >}}n_1$ and $n_2$ which are separated by a spherical surface. Let $C$ be the centre of curvature of the spherical surface. Let a point object $O$ be in the medium $n_1$. The line $OC$ is the principal axis that cuts the spherical surface at the pole $P{{< /katex >}}. As the rays considered are paraxial rays, the perpendicular dropped from the point of incidence to the principal axis is very close to the pole (or) passes through the pole itself.

![Refraction at single spherical surface](image-placeholder)

Light from {{< katex display=true >}}O$ falls on the refracting surface at $N$. The normal drawn to the refracting surface at the point of incidence passes through the centre of curvature $C$. As $n_2 > n_1$, light in the denser medium deviates towards the normal and meets the principal axis at $I{{< /katex >}} where the image is formed.

Snell's law in product form for the refraction at the point {{< katex display=true >}}N{{< /katex >}} can be written from equation (6.19),

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
n_1 \sin i = n_2 \sin r
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

As the angles are small, {{< katex display=true >}}\sin i \approx i$, $\sin r \approx r{{< /katex >}},

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
n_1 i = n_2 r \tag{6.51}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

Let the angles be,
{{< katex display=true >}}\angle NOP = \alpha$, $\angle NCP = \beta$, $\angle NIP = \gamma{{< /katex >}}.

From the right angle triangles {{< katex display=true >}}\Delta NOP$, $\Delta NCP$ and $\Delta NIP{{< /katex >}},

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\tan \alpha = \frac{PN}{PO}, \quad \tan \beta = \frac{PN}{PC}, \quad \tan \gamma = \frac{PN}{PI}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

As these angles are small, {{< katex display=true >}}\tan \alpha \approx \alpha{{< /katex >}}, etc.

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\alpha = \frac{PN}{PO}, \quad \beta = \frac{PN}{PC}, \quad \gamma = \frac{PN}{PI} \tag{6.52}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

For the triangle {{< katex display=true >}}\Delta ONC$, $i = \alpha + \beta{{< /katex >}} \tag{6.53}
For the triangle {{< katex display=true >}}\Delta INC$, $\beta = r + \gamma$ or $r = \beta - \gamma{{< /katex >}} \tag{6.54}

Substituting {{< katex display=true >}}i$ and $r{{< /katex >}} from (6.53) and (6.54) into (6.51),

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
n_1(\alpha + \beta) = n_2(\beta - \gamma)
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

After rearranging,

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
n_1\alpha + n_2\gamma = (n_2 - n_1)\beta
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

Substituting for {{< katex display=true >}}\alpha, \beta, \gamma{{< /katex >}} from (6.52),

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
n_1\left(\frac{PN}{PO}\right) + n_2\left(\frac{PN}{PI}\right) = (n_2 - n_1)\left(\frac{PN}{PC}\right)
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

Cancelling {{< katex display=true >}}PN{{< /katex >}},

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\frac{n_1}{PO} + \frac{n_2}{PI} = \frac{n_2 - n_1}{PC} \tag{6.55}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

Using sign conventions: {{< katex display=true >}}PO = -u$, $PI = +v$, $PC = +R{{< /katex >}}. Substituting,

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\frac{n_1}{-u} + \frac{n_2}{v} = \frac{n_2 - n_1}{R}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

After rearranging,

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\frac{n_2}{v} - \frac{n_1}{u} = \frac{n_2 - n_1}{R} \tag{6.56}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

If the first medium is air, then {{< katex display=true >}}n_1 = 1$ and $n_2 = n{{< /katex >}},

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\frac{n}{v} - \frac{1}{u} = \frac{n - 1}{R} \tag{6.57}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

---

**EXAMPLE 6.11**  
Find the position of the image of a point object O in the two cases given. Take the radius of curvature of the surface {{< katex display=true >}}R$ as $15\ \mathrm{cm}$, $n_1 = 1$ and $n_2 = 2{{< /katex >}}.  
Case i) O is located {{< katex display=true >}}10\ \mathrm{cm}{{< /katex >}} to the left of the surface.  
Case ii) O is located {{< katex display=true >}}30\ \mathrm{cm}{{< /katex >}} to the left of the surface.

**Solution**  
Case i) {{< katex display=true >}}\frac{n_2}{v} - \frac{n_1}{u} = \frac{n_2 - n_1}{R}{{< /katex >}}  
Applying sign convention: {{< katex display=true >}}u = -10\ \mathrm{cm}$, $R = 15\ \mathrm{cm}{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\frac{2}{v} - \frac{1}{(-10)} = \frac{2 - 1}{15} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \frac{2}{v} + \frac{1}{10} = \frac{1}{15}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\frac{2}{v} = \frac{1}{15} - \frac{1}{10} = \frac{2 - 3}{30} = -\frac{1}{30} \quad \Rightarrow \quad v = -60\ \mathrm{cm}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

A virtual image is formed {{< katex display=true >}}60\ \mathrm{cm}{{< /katex >}} to the left of the surface.

Case ii) {{< katex display=true >}}u = -30\ \mathrm{cm}$, $R = 15\ \mathrm{cm}{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\frac{2}{v} - \frac{1}{(-30)} = \frac{1}{15} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \frac{2}{v} + \frac{1}{30} = \frac{1}{15}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\frac{2}{v} = \frac{1}{15} - \frac{1}{30} = \frac{1}{30} \quad \Rightarrow \quad v = 60\ \mathrm{cm}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

A real image is formed {{< katex display=true >}}60\ \mathrm{cm}{{< /katex >}} to the right of the surface.

## 6.6 THIN LENS

A lens is formed by a transparent material bounded between two spherical surfaces (or) one plane and another spherical surface. In a thin lens, the distance between the surfaces is very small. For two spherical surfaces, there will be two centres of curvature {{< katex display=true >}}C_1$ and $C_2$ and correspondingly two radii of curvature $R_1$ and $R_2$. For a plane surface, the centre of curvature $C$ is at infinity and its radius of curvature $R$ is infinity $(R = \infty){{< /katex >}}. The terminologies of spherical mirrors hold good very much for thin lenses except for the focal length.

### 6.6.1 Primary and secondary focus

As the thin lens is formed by two surfaces, the lens may separate two different media. i.e. the media to the left and right of the lens may be different. Hence, we have two focal lengths.

The primary focus {{< katex display=true >}}F_1$ is defined as a point where a point source kept produces a parallel emergent rays to the principal axis after passing through lens. For a converging lens, such an object is a real object and for a diverging lens, it is a virtual object. The distance $PF_1$ is the primary focal length $f_1{{< /katex >}}.

![Primary focus](image-placeholder)

The secondary focus {{< katex display=true >}}F_2$ is defined as a point where all the parallel rays travelling close to the principal axis converge to form an image on the principal axis after passing through lens. For a converging lens, such an image is a real image and for a diverging lens, it is a virtual image. The distance $PF_2$ is the secondary focal length $f_2{{< /katex >}}.

![Secondary focus](image-placeholder)

If the media on the two sides of a thin lens have same refractive index, then the two focal lengths are equal. We will mostly be using the secondary focus {{< katex display=true >}}F_2{{< /katex >}} in our further discussions.

### 6.6.2 Sign conventions on focal length for lens

The sign conventions for thin lenses differ only in the signs followed for focal lengths.
(i) The sign of focal length is not decided on the direction of measurement of the focal length from the pole of the lens as they have two focal lengths, one to the left and another to the right.
(ii) The focal length of the thin lens is taken as positive for a converging lens and negative for a diverging lens.

The other sign conventions for object distance, image distance, radius of curvature, object height and image height (except for the focal lengths as mentioned above) remain the same for thin lenses as that of spherical mirrors.

### 6.6.3 Lens maker's formula and lens equation

Let us consider a thin lens made up of a medium of refractive index {{< katex display=true >}}n_2$ placed in a medium of refractive index $n_1$. Let $R_1$ and $R_2$ be the radii of curvature of two spherical surfaces ① and ② respectively and $P$ be the pole. Consider a point object $O$ on the principal axis. A paraxial ray from $O$ which falls very close to $P$, after refraction at the surface ① forms image at $I'$. Before it does so, it is again refracted by the surface ②. Therefore, the final image is formed at $I{{< /katex >}}.

![Refraction through thin lens](image-placeholder)

The general equation for the refraction at a single spherical surface is given by equation (6.56),

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\frac{n_2}{v} - \frac{n_1}{u} = \frac{n_2 - n_1}{R}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

For the refracting surface ①, the light goes from {{< katex display=true >}}n_1$ to $n_2{{< /katex >}},

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\frac{n_2}{v'} - \frac{n_1}{u} = \frac{n_2 - n_1}{R_1} \tag{6.58}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

For the refracting surface ②, the light goes from medium {{< katex display=true >}}n_2$ to $n_1{{< /katex >}},

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\frac{n_1}{v} - \frac{n_2}{v'} = \frac{n_1 - n_2}{R_2} \tag{6.59}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

For surface ②, {{< katex display=true >}}I'{{< /katex >}} acts as virtual object.

Adding equations (6.58) and (6.59),

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\frac{n_1}{v} - \frac{n_1}{u} = (n_2 - n_1)\left(\frac{1}{R_1} - \frac{1}{R_2}\right)
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

On further simplifying and rearranging,

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\frac{1}{v} - \frac{1}{u} = \left(\frac{n_2 - n_1}{n_1}\right)\left(\frac{1}{R_1} - \frac{1}{R_2}\right) \tag{6.60}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

If the object is at infinity, the image is formed at the focus of the lens. Thus, for {{< katex display=true >}}u = \infty$, $v = f{{< /katex >}}. Then the equation becomes,

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\frac{1}{f} = \left(\frac{n_2}{n_1} - 1\right)\left(\frac{1}{R_1} - \frac{1}{R_2}\right) \tag{6.61}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

If the lens is kept in air, then {{< katex display=true >}}n_1 = 1$ and $n_2 = n{{< /katex >}}. So,

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\frac{1}{f} = (n - 1)\left(\frac{1}{R_1} - \frac{1}{R_2}\right) \tag{6.62}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

The above formula is called the lens maker's formula. By comparing equations (6.60) and (6.61) we can write,

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\frac{1}{v} - \frac{1}{u} = \frac{1}{f} \tag{6.63}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

This is the lens equation which relates object distance {{< katex display=true >}}u$ and image distance $v$ with the focal length $f{{< /katex >}} of the lens.

### 6.6.4 Lateral magnification in thin lens

Let us consider an object {{< katex display=true >}}OO'$ of height $h_1$ placed on the principal axis with its height perpendicular to the principal axis. The inverted real image $II'$ is formed which has a height $h_2{{< /katex >}}.

![Lateral magnification in thin lens](image-placeholder)

The lateral (or) transverse magnification {{< katex display=true >}}m{{< /katex >}} is defined as the ratio of the height of the image to height of the object.

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
m = \frac{II'}{OO'} \tag{6.64}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

From the two similar triangles {{< katex display=true >}}\Delta POO'$ and $\Delta PII'{{< /katex >}}, we can write,

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\frac{II'}{OO'} = \frac{PI}{PO} \tag{6.65}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

Applying sign convention, {{< katex display=true >}}\frac{-h'}{h} = \frac{v}{-u}{{< /katex >}}. Substituting in (6.65),

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
m = \frac{h'}{h} = \frac{v}{u} \tag{6.66}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

The magnification is negative for real image and positive for virtual image. In the case of a concave lens, the magnification is always positive and less than one. Other forms by combining lens equation:

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
m = \frac{f}{f + u} = \frac{f - v}{f} \tag{6.67}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

**EXAMPLE 6.12**  
A biconvex lens has radii of curvature {{< katex display=true >}}20\ \mathrm{cm}$ and $15\ \mathrm{cm}{{< /katex >}} for the two curved surfaces. The refractive index of the material of the lens is 1.5.  
(a) What is its focal length?  
(b) Will the focal length change if the lens is flipped by the side?

**Solution**  
For a biconvex lens, radius of curvature of the first surface is positive and that of the second surface is negative.  
Given, {{< katex display=true >}}n = 1.5$, $R_1 = 20\ \mathrm{cm}$, $R_2 = -15\ \mathrm{cm}{{< /katex >}}.

(a) Lens maker's formula: {{< katex display=true >}}\frac{1}{f} = (n - 1)\left(\frac{1}{R_1} - \frac{1}{R_2}\right){{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

### 6.6.5 Power of a lens

The power of a lens is a measure of its deviating ability on an incident light. When a ray is incident on a lens then the degree with which the lens deviates the ray is determined by the power of the lens. Power of the lens is inversely proportional to the focal length i.e., greater the power of lens, smaller will be the focal length. In other words, the power of a lens is a measure of the degree of convergence (or) divergence the lens produces on the light falling on it.

The power of a lens {{< katex display=true >}}P{{< /katex >}} is the reciprocal of its focal length in meter.

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
P = \frac{1}{f} \tag{6.68}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

The unit of power is dioptre (D). {{< katex display=true >}}1\ \mathrm{D} = 1\ \mathrm{m^{-1}}{{< /katex >}}. Power is positive for converging lens and negative for diverging lens.

From the lens maker's formula,

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
P = \frac{1}{f} = (n - 1)\left(\frac{1}{R_1} - \frac{1}{R_2}\right) \tag{6.69}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

### 6.6.6 Focal length of lenses in contact

Let us consider two lenses ① and ② of focal lengths {{< katex display=true >}}f_1$ and $f_2$ placed coaxially in contact with each other so that they have a common principal axis. For lens ②, object distance {{< katex display=true >}}PI' = v'$, image distance $PI = v{{< /katex >}}.

Lens equation for lens ①:

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

Lens equation for lens ②:

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\frac{1}{v} - \frac{1}{v'} = \frac{1}{f_2} \tag{6.71}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

Adding (6.70) and (6.71):

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

The combination acts as a single lens of focal length {{< katex display=true >}}f{{< /katex >}} so that

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

Comparing (6.72) and (6.73):

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{f_1} + \frac{1}{f_2} \tag{6.74}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

For any number of lenses in contact,

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{f_1} + \frac{1}{f_2} + \frac{1}{f_3} + \cdots \tag{6.75}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

In terms of power,

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
P = P_1 + P_2 + P_3 + \cdots \tag{6.76}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

The total magnification {{< katex display=true >}}m{{< /katex >}} of the combination is the product of magnifications of individual lenses:

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

### 6.6.7 Silvered lenses

If one of the surfaces of a lens is silvered from outside, then such a lens is said to be a silvered lens. A silvered lens is a combination of a lens and a mirror. ![Silvered lens](image-placeholder)

The power {{< katex display=true >}}P{{< /katex >}} of the silvered lens is,

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
P = P_l + P_m + P_l = 2P_l + P_m \tag{6.78}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

Here {{< katex display=true >}}P_l$ is the power of the lens and $P_m$ is the power of the mirror. The power of a lens is $\frac{1}{f_l}$, and the power of a mirror is $-\frac{1}{f_m}$ (since concave mirror has negative focal length and is considered to have positive power? Actually careful: For a mirror, the power is defined as $P_m = -\frac{1}{f_m}$ (in dioptres) because the mirror equation is $\frac{1}{v} + \frac{1}{u} = \frac{1}{f}$ and for a concave mirror $f$ is negative, so $P_m{{< /katex >}} becomes positive. But in the context of silvered lens, we use the formula as given.

Thus,

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\frac{1}{-f} = \frac{2}{f_l} + \frac{1}{-f_m} \tag{6.80}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

Proper sign conventions are to be followed. Alternatively, we can treat the silvered lens as a mirror and use the mirror equation directly.

## 6.7 PRISM

A prism is a triangular block of transparent glass. It is bounded by the three plane faces. Its one face is rough which is called base of the prism. The other two faces are polished which are called refracting faces of the prism. The angle between the two refracting faces is called angle of the prism (or) refracting angle (or) apex angle. It is represented as {{< katex display=true >}}A{{< /katex >}}.

![Prism](image-placeholder)

### 6.7.1 Angle of deviation produced by prism

Consider a prism ABC. The faces AB and AC are polished and the face BC is rough. Let light ray PQ is incident on one of the refracting faces of the prism. The angles of incidence and refraction at the first face AB are {{< katex display=true >}}i_1$ and $r_1$. The path of the light inside the prism is QR. The angles of incidence and refraction at the second face AC are $r_2$ and $i_2$ respectively. RS is the ray emerging from the second face. Angle $i_2$ is also called as angle of emergence. The angle between the direction of incident ray and the emergent ray is called the angle of deviation $d{{< /katex >}} in a prism.

![Refraction through prism](image-placeholder)

The angle of deviation {{< katex display=true >}}d_1$ at surface AB is $d_1 = i_1 - r_1{{< /katex >}}.  
The angle of deviation {{< katex display=true >}}d_2$ at surface AC is $d_2 = i_2 - r_2{{< /katex >}}.  
Total angle of deviation {{< katex display=true >}}d = d_1 + d_2 = (i_1 + i_2) - (r_1 + r_2){{< /katex >}}.

In the quadrilateral AQNR, two angles are right angles, so {{< katex display=true >}}\angle A + \angle QNR = 180^\circ$. In triangle QNR, $r_1 + r_2 + \angle QNR = 180^\circ$. Comparing, we get $r_1 + r_2 = A{{< /katex >}}.

Thus,

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
d = i_1 + i_2 - A \tag{6.88}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

The angle of deviation depends on the angle of incidence {{< katex display=true >}}i_1$, the angle of emergence $i_2$, and the angle of the prism $A{{< /katex >}}. But for a given angle of incidence, the angle of emergence is determined by the refractive index of the material of the prism.

---

Since the prism is equilateral, {{< katex display=true >}}A = 60^\circ$. Given $i_1 = 30^\circ$, $i_2 = 75^\circ{{< /katex >}}.

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

(a) What is the angle of deviation produced?  
(b) What is the refractive index of the material of the prism?

**Solution**  
Given {{< katex display=true >}}A = 60^\circ$, $i_1 = 0^\circ$, $i_2 = 90^\circ{{< /katex >}}.

(a) {{< katex display=true >}}d = i_1 + i_2 - A = 0^\circ + 90^\circ - 60^\circ = 30^\circ{{< /katex >}}.

(b) The light inside the prism must be falling on the second face at critical angle as it graces the boundary. In the prism, the ray travels parallel to the base? For an equilateral prism, when the ray grazes the second face, the angle of incidence on that face is the critical angle. The geometry: Inside the prism, the angle of refraction at first face: {{< katex display=true >}}r_1 = ?$ Since $i_1 = 0$, $r_1 = 0$. Then the ray travels along the base? Actually if the ray enters normally, it goes straight to the second face. The angle of incidence at the second face is then the angle between the ray and the normal to that face. For an equilateral prism, the base angles are $60^\circ$. The ray from the first face goes horizontally (if the prism is oriented with base horizontal). The normal to the second face makes an angle of $60^\circ$ with the base. Thus the angle of incidence at the second face is $60^\circ$. So $i_c = 60^\circ{{< /katex >}}.

Thus {{< katex display=true >}}n = \frac{1}{\sin i_c} = \frac{1}{\sin 60^\circ} = \frac{2}{\sqrt{3}} \approx 1.15{{< /katex >}}.

### 6.7.2 Angle of minimum deviation

A graph plotted between the angle of incidence and angle of deviation shows that the angle of deviation decreases with increase in angle of incidence and reaches a minimum value {{< katex display=true >}}D{{< /katex >}} and then continues to increase.

![Graph between {{< katex display=true >}}i$ and $d{{< /katex >}}](image-placeholder)

The minimum value of angle of deviation is called angle of minimum deviation {{< katex display=true >}}D{{< /katex >}}. At minimum deviation,
(i) the angle of incidence is equal to the angle of emergence, {{< katex display=true >}}i_1 = i_2 = i{{< /katex >}}.
(ii) the angle of refraction at the face one and face two are equal, {{< katex display=true >}}r_1 = r_2 = r{{< /katex >}}.
(iii) the refracted ray inside the prism is parallel to its base of the prism.

### 6.7.3 Refractive index of the material of the prism

At minimum deviation,
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
i = \frac{A + D}{2}, \quad r = \frac{A}{2}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

Snell's law gives

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
n = \frac{\sin i}{\sin r} = \frac{\sin\left(\frac{A + D}{2}\right)}{\sin\left(\frac{A}{2}\right)} \tag{6.89}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

### 6.7.4 Dispersion of white light through prism

When white light enters a prism, an effect called dispersion takes place. Dispersion is splitting of white light into its constituent colours. This band of colours of light is called its spectrum. When a narrow beam of parallel rays of white light is incident on a prism, a band of colours is obtained in the order VIBGYOR (Violet, Indigo, Blue, Green, Yellow, Orange, Red). Violet is the most deviated colour and red is the least deviated.

![Dispersion of white light into its constituent colours](image-placeholder)

Each colour in vacuum has a definite wavelength. Red has the longest wavelength of about {{< katex display=true >}}700\ \mathrm{nm}$ while violet has the shortest wavelength of about $400\ \mathrm{nm}{{< /katex >}}. All colours travel with the same speed in vacuum. In a medium, the red colour travels with the highest speed and violet colour with the least speed. Hence, the refractive index of the material of the prism is different for different colours. For violet, the refractive index is the highest and for red it is the least.

**Table 6.4 Refractive indices for different wavelengths**

| Colour | Wavelength in vacuum (nm) | Crown glass | Flint glass |
|--------|---------------------------|-------------|-------------|
| Violet | 396.9 | 1.533 | 1.663 |
| Blue | 486.1 | 1.523 | 1.639 |
| Yellow | 589.3 | 1.517 | 1.627 |
| Red | 656.3 | 1.515 | 1.622 |

### 6.7.5 Dispersive Power

Consider a beam of white light passing through a prism. If the angle of prism is small (of the order of {{< katex display=true >}}10^\circ$), the prism is said to be a small angle prism. For a small angle prism, the angle of minimum deviation also becomes small. For small angles, $\sin \theta \approx \theta$. Then from (6.89), $n \approx \frac{(A+D)/2}{A/2} = \frac{A+D}{A} = 1 + \frac{D}{A}{{< /katex >}}, so

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
D = (n - 1)A \tag{6.91}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

Let {{< katex display=true >}}\delta_v$ and $\delta_R$ be the angles of minimum deviation for violet and red colour, and $n_v$ and $n_R{{< /katex >}} the corresponding refractive indices. Then

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\delta_v = (n_v - 1)A, \quad \delta_R = (n_R - 1)A
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

The angular dispersion between the two extreme colours is {{< katex display=true >}}\delta_v - \delta_R = (n_v - n_R)A{{< /katex >}}.

If {{< katex display=true >}}\delta$ is the angle of minimum deviation for any mean colour (green or yellow) and $n$ the corresponding refractive index, then $\delta = (n - 1)A{{< /katex >}}.

Dispersive power {{< katex display=true >}}\omega{{< /katex >}} is defined as the ratio of the angular dispersion to the deviation for the mean colour:

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\omega = \frac{\delta_v - \delta_R}{\delta} = \frac{n_v - n_R}{n - 1} \tag{6.97}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

---

**EXAMPLE 6.21**  
Find the dispersive power of a prism if the refractive indices of flint glass for red, green and violet colours are 1.613, 1.620 and 1.632 respectively.

**Solution**  
{{< katex display=true >}}n_v = 1.632$, $n_R = 1.613$, $n_G = 1.620{{< /katex >}}.

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
\omega = \frac{n_v - n_R}{n_G - 1} = \frac{1.632 - 1.613}{1.620 - 1} = \frac{0.019}{0.620} = 0.0306
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

### 6.7.6 Scattering of sunlight

When sunlight enters the atmosphere of earth, the particles present in the atmosphere change the direction of the light. This process is known as scattering of light.

If the scattering of light is by atoms and molecules which have size much less than the wavelength {{< katex display=true >}}\lambda$ of light ($a \ll \lambda{{< /katex >}}), then the scattering is called Rayleigh scattering. The intensity of light scattered in Rayleigh scattering is inversely proportional to the fourth power of wavelength:

{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}
I \propto \frac{1}{\lambda^4} \tag{6.98}
{{< katex display=true >}}{{< /katex >}}

During day time, violet colour which has the shortest wavelength gets scattered the most. The next scattered colour is blue. As our eyes are more sensitive to blue colour than violet, the sky appears blue during day time. During sunrise and sunset, the light from the sun travels a greater distance through the atmosphere. Hence, blue light (shorter wavelength) is scattered away and red light (longer wavelength, less scattered) manages to reach our eye. This is the reason for the reddish appearance of sky during sunrise and sunset.

If light is scattered by large particles like dust and water droplets which have size greater than the wavelength ({{< katex display=true >}}a \gg \lambda{{< /katex >}}), the intensity of scattering is equal for all colours. This non-Rayleigh scattering is independent of wavelength. This happens in clouds, giving them a whitish appearance. Rain clouds appear dark because of condensation of water droplets on dust particles making the cloud opaque.

If earth had no atmosphere, there would be no scattering and the sky would appear dark. That is why sky appears dark for astronauts above the atmosphere.

## SUMMARY

- A ray of light gives the direction of light.
- Law of reflection is {{< katex display=true >}}i = r{{< /katex >}}.
- Paraxial rays are the rays travelling close to the principal axis of the mirror and make small angles with it.
- The relation between focal length and radius of curvature in spherical mirror is {{< katex display=true >}}2f = R$ (or) $f = \frac{R}{2}{{< /katex >}}.
- Cartesian sign conventions are to be followed to trace image formed by spherical mirrors.
- The mirror equation is {{< katex display=true >}}\frac{1}{v} + \frac{1}{u} = \frac{1}{f}{{< /katex >}}.
- The magnification in spherical mirror is {{< katex display=true >}}m = \frac{h'}{h} = -\frac{v}{u}$, $m = \frac{f - v}{f} = \frac{f}{f - u}{{< /katex >}}.
- Light travels with lesser velocity in optically denser medium.
- Refractive index is the ratio of speed of light in vacuum to speed of light in medium, {{< katex display=true >}}n = \frac{c}{v}{{< /katex >}}.
- Optical path is the equivalent path travelled in vacuum in the same time light travels through a optically denser medium: {{< katex display=true >}}d' = nd{{< /katex >}}.
- Law of refraction (Snell's law) in ratio form: {{< katex display=true >}}\frac{\sin i}{\sin r} = \frac{n_2}{n_1}$; in product form: $n_1 \sin i = n_2 \sin r{{< /katex >}}.
- The relative refractive index of second medium with respect to first medium is {{< katex display=true >}}n_{21} = \frac{n_2}{n_1}{{< /katex >}}.
- The apparent depth is always lesser than actual depth: {{< katex display=true >}}d' = \frac{d}{n}{{< /katex >}}.
- Critical angle {{< katex display=true >}}i_c$ is the angle of incidence for which the angle of refraction is $90^\circ$. For $i > i_c$, total internal reflection occurs. $\sin i_c = \frac{1}{n}{{< /katex >}}.
- Snell's window radius: {{< katex display=true >}}R = \frac{d}{\sqrt{n^2 - 1}}{{< /katex >}}.
- Optical fibre makes use of total internal reflection. Acceptance angle: {{< katex display=true >}}i_a = \sin^{-1}\left(\sqrt{n_1^2 - n_2^2}\right)$; Numerical aperture: $\mathrm{NA} = \sqrt{n_1^2 - n_2^2}{{< /katex >}}.
- Lateral displacement in glass slab: {{< katex display=true >}}L = t \frac{\sin(i - r)}{\cos r}{{< /katex >}}.
- Refraction at single spherical surface: {{< katex display=true >}}\frac{n_2}{v} - \frac{n_1}{u} = \frac{n_2 - n_1}{R}{{< /katex >}}.
- Lens maker's formula: {{< katex display=true >}}\frac{1}{f} = (n - 1)\left(\frac{1}{R_1} - \frac{1}{R_2}\right){{< /katex >}}.
- Lens equation: {{< katex display=true >}}\frac{1}{v} - \frac{1}{u} = \frac{1}{f}{{< /katex >}}.
- Magnification by lens: {{< katex display=true >}}m = \frac{v}{u} = \frac{f}{f+u} = \frac{f-v}{f}{{< /katex >}}.
- Power of a lens: {{< katex display=true >}}P = \frac{1}{f}{{< /katex >}} (in meters). Unit: dioptre (D).
- Effective focal length of lenses in contact: {{< katex display=true >}}\frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{f_1} + \frac{1}{f_2}{{< /katex >}}.
- Angle of deviation in prism: {{< katex display=true >}}d = i_1 + i_2 - A{{< /katex >}}.
- At minimum deviation: {{< katex display=true >}}i_1 = i_2 = i$, $r_1 = r_2 = r = A/2$, and $n = \frac{\sin\left(\frac{A+D}{2}\right)}{\sin\left(\frac{A}{2}\right)}{{< /katex >}}.
- Dispersive power: {{< katex display=true >}}\omega = \frac{n_v - n_R}{n - 1}{{< /katex >}}.
- Rayleigh scattering: {{< katex display=true >}}I \propto 1/\lambda^4{{< /katex >}}.
