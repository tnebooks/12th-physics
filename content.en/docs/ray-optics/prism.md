A prism is a triangular block of transparent glass. It is bounded by the three plane faces. Its one face is rough which is called base of the prism. The other two faces are polished which are called refracting faces of the prism. The angle between the two refracting faces is called angle of the prism (or) refracting angle (or) apex angle. It is represented as \(A\).



### Angle of deviation produced by prism

Consider a prism ABC. The faces AB and AC are polished and the face BC is rough. Let light ray PQ is incident on one of the refracting faces of the prism. The angles of incidence and refraction at the first face AB are \(i_1\) and \(r_1\). The path of the light inside the prism is QR. The angles of incidence and refraction at the second face AC are \(r_2\) and \(i_2\) respectively. RS is the ray emerging from the second face. Angle \(i_2\) is also called as angle of emergence. The angle between the direction of incident ray and the emergent ray is called the angle of deviation \(d\) in a prism.



The angle of deviation \(d_1\) at surface AB is \(d_1 = i_1 - r_1\).  
The angle of deviation \(d_2\) at surface AC is \(d_2 = i_2 - r_2\).  
Total angle of deviation \(d = d_1 + d_2 = (i_1 + i_2) - (r_1 + r_2)\).

In the quadrilateral AQNR, two angles are right angles, so \(\angle A + \angle QNR = 180^\circ\). In triangle QNR, \(r_1 + r_2 + \angle QNR = 180^\circ\). Comparing, we get \(r_1 + r_2 = A\).

Thus,

$$
d = i_1 + i_2 - A \tag{6.88}
$$

The angle of deviation depends on the angle of incidence \(i_1\), the angle of emergence \(i_2\), and the angle of the prism A. But for a given angle of incidence, the angle of emergence is determined by the refractive index of the material of the prism.

---

**EXAMPLE 6.18**  
A monochromatic light is incident on an equilateral prism at an angle \(30^\circ\) and is emergent at an angle of \(75^\circ\). What is the angle of deviation produced by the prism?

**Solution**  
Since the prism is equilateral, \(A = 60^\circ\). Given \(i_1 = 30^\circ\), \(i_2 = 75^\circ\).

$$
d = i_1 + i_2 - A = 30^\circ + 75^\circ - 60^\circ = 45^\circ
$$

---

**EXAMPLE 6.19**  
Light ray falls at normal incidence on the first face and emerges gracing the second face for an equilateral prism.  
(a) What is the angle of deviation produced?  
(b) What is the refractive index of the material of the prism?

**Solution**  
Given \(A = 60^\circ\), \(i_1 = 0^\circ\), \(i_2 = 90^\circ\).

(a) \(d = i_1 + i_2 - A = 0^\circ + 90^\circ - 60^\circ = 30^\circ\).

(b) The light inside the prism must be falling on the second face at critical angle as it graces the boundary. In the prism, the ray travels parallel to the base? For an equilateral prism, when the ray grazes the second face, the angle of incidence on that face is the critical angle. The geometry: Inside the prism, the angle of refraction at first face: \(r_1 = ?\) Since \(i_1 = 0\), \(r_1 = 0\). Then the ray travels along the base? Actually if the ray enters normally, it goes straight to the second face. The angle of incidence at the second face is then the angle between the ray and the normal to that face. For an equilateral prism, the base angles are \(60^\circ\). The ray from the first face goes horizontally (if the prism is oriented with base horizontal). The normal to the second face makes an angle of \(60^\circ\) with the base. Thus the angle of incidence at the second face is \(60^\circ\). So \(i_c = 60^\circ\).

Thus \(n = \frac{1}{\sin i_c} = \frac{1}{\sin 60^\circ} = \frac{2}{\sqrt{3}} \approx 1.15\).

### Angle of minimum deviation

A graph plotted between the angle of incidence and angle of deviation shows that the angle of deviation decreases with increase in angle of incidence and reaches a minimum value D and then continues to increase.



The minimum value of angle of deviation is called angle of minimum deviation D. At minimum deviation,
(i) the angle of incidence is equal to the angle of emergence, \(i_1 = i_2 = i\).
(ii) the angle of refraction at the face one and face two are equal, \(r_1 = r_2 = r\).
(iii) the refracted ray inside the prism is parallel to its base of the prism.



### Refractive index of the material of the prism

At minimum deviation,
$$
i = \frac{A + D}{2}, \quad r = \frac{A}{2}
$$

Snell's law gives

$$
n = \frac{\sin i}{\sin r} = \frac{\sin\left(\frac{A + D}{2}\right)}{\sin\left(\frac{A}{2}\right)} \tag{6.89}
$$

---

**EXAMPLE 6.20**  
The angle of minimum deviation for an equilateral prism is \(37^\circ\). Find the refractive index of the material of the prism.

**Solution**  
\(A = 60^\circ\), \(D = 37^\circ\).

$$
n = \frac{\sin\left(\frac{60+37}{2}\right)}{\sin\left(\frac{60}{2}\right)} = \frac{\sin 48.5^\circ}{\sin 30^\circ} = \frac{0.7480}{0.5} = 1.496 \approx 1.5
$$

### Dispersion of white light through prism

When white light enters a prism, an effect called dispersion takes place. Dispersion is splitting of white light into its constituent colours. This band of colours of light is called its spectrum. When a narrow beam of parallel rays of white light is incident on a prism, a band of colours is obtained in the order VIBGYOR (Violet, Indigo, Blue, Green, Yellow, Orange, Red). Violet is the most deviated colour and red is the least deviated.



Each colour in vacuum has a definite wavelength. Red has the longest wavelength of about \(700\ \mathrm{nm}\) while violet has the shortest wavelength of about \(400\ \mathrm{nm}\). All colours travel with the same speed in vacuum. In a medium, the red colour travels with the highest speed and violet colour with the least speed. Hence, the refractive index of the material of the prism is different for different colours. For violet, the refractive index is the highest and for red it is the least.

**Table 6.4 Refractive indices for different wavelengths**

| Colour | Wavelength in vacuum (nm) | Crown glass | Flint glass |
|--------|---------------------------|-------------|-------------|
| Violet | 396.9 | 1.533 | 1.663 |
| Blue | 486.1 | 1.523 | 1.639 |
| Yellow | 589.3 | 1.517 | 1.627 |
| Red | 656.3 | 1.515 | 1.622 |

### Dispersive Power

Consider a beam of white light passing through a prism. If the angle of prism is small (of the order of \(10^\circ\)), the prism is said to be a small angle prism. For a small angle prism, the angle of minimum deviation also becomes small. For small angles, \(\sin \theta \approx \theta\). Then from (6.89), \(n \approx \frac{(A+D)/2}{A/2} = \frac{A+D}{A} = 1 + \frac{D}{A}\), so

$$
D = (n - 1)A \tag{6.91}
$$

Let \(\delta_v\) and \(\delta_R\) be the angles of minimum deviation for violet and red colour, and \(n_v\) and \(n_R\) the corresponding refractive indices. Then

$$
\delta_v = (n_v - 1)A, \quad \delta_R = (n_R - 1)A
$$

The angular dispersion between the two extreme colours is \(\delta_v - \delta_R = (n_v - n_R)A\).

If \(\delta\) is the angle of minimum deviation for any mean colour (green or yellow) and \(n\) the corresponding refractive index, then \(\delta = (n - 1)A\).

Dispersive power \(\omega\) is defined as the ratio of the angular dispersion to the deviation for the mean colour:

$$
\omega = \frac{\delta_v - \delta_R}{\delta} = \frac{n_v - n_R}{n - 1} \tag{6.97}
$$

---

**EXAMPLE 6.21**  
Find the dispersive power of a prism if the refractive indices of flint glass for red, green and violet colours are 1.613, 1.620 and 1.632 respectively.

**Solution**  
\(n_v = 1.632\), \(n_R = 1.613\), \(n_G = 1.620\).

$$
\omega = \frac{n_v - n_R}{n_G - 1} = \frac{1.632 - 1.613}{1.620 - 1} = \frac{0.019}{0.620} = 0.0306
$$

### Scattering of sunlight

When sunlight enters the atmosphere of earth, the particles present in the atmosphere change the direction of the light. This process is known as scattering of light.

If the scattering of light is by atoms and molecules which have size much less than the wavelength \(\lambda\) of light (\(a \ll \lambda\)), then the scattering is called Rayleigh scattering. The intensity of light scattered in Rayleigh scattering is inversely proportional to the fourth power of wavelength:

$$
I \propto \frac{1}{\lambda^4} \tag{6.98}
$$

During day time, violet colour which has the shortest wavelength gets scattered the most. The next scattered colour is blue. As our eyes are more sensitive to blue colour than violet, the sky appears blue during day time. During sunrise and sunset, the light from the sun travels a greater distance through the atmosphere. Hence, blue light (shorter wavelength) is scattered away and red light (longer wavelength, less scattered) manages to reach our eye. This is the reason for the reddish appearance of sky during sunrise and sunset.

If light is scattered by large particles like dust and water droplets which have size greater than the wavelength (\(a \gg \lambda\)), the intensity of scattering is equal for all colours. This non-Rayleigh scattering is independent of wavelength. This happens in clouds, giving them a whitish appearance. Rain clouds appear dark because of condensation of water droplets on dust particles making the cloud opaque.

If earth had no atmosphere, there would be no scattering and the sky would appear dark. That is why sky appears dark for astronauts above the atmosphere.
